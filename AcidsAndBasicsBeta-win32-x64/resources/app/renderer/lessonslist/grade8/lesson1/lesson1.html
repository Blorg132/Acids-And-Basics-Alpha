Now, it's time for your first lesson!

Chemistry is the study of matter, and how it changes within our physical world. Matter is essentially the core material that everything is made out of; your TV, your food, air, and even your own body, are all made out of matter! Imagine matter as the Play-Doh of the world. Everything physical can be made out of it, and IS made out of it. You might notice however, not everything looks the same! More importantly, things don't necessarily act the same either! So why is that?

There's a lot of things that seperate water from, let's say, a table. Number one being, water isn't made out of wood, metal, or any other table substance you can think of. This is because the elements that make up the table are different from the elements that make up water. The second thing being that water flows, it moves around, and you're welcome to dive in it at any time. If you've ever tried diving into a table, aside from maybe breaking your back and getting a fail video of yourself on Reddit, the experience couldn't be more different. This because matter, even when the elements are the exact same, can exist in different physical states, also known as the different states of matter. Water can be both in liquid form, gas form (steam), and solid form (ice), but in chemistry, we consider all as "water", just in different states/modes.

Alright, now I want you to imagine that we took that liquid water from before, and put it in a bowl with a crushed and broken table leg from our earlier diving session. Does the bowl only hold water? No! It holds water, and part of that table. This is how mixtures generally work. It's when we put one seperate piece of matter along with another seperate piece of matter, and mix it all up. In the case of our mixture, it's pretty obvious to see we put a table leg in it. This is because, despite it all being mixed, we can CLEARLY see that something else is in that bowl. This is called a heterogenous mixture! However, if I take some salt and mix it in another bowl of water, once it completely dissolves, you can't even tell I added it in the first place. When we can't differentiate between the two pieces of matter, it's called a homogenous mixture!

One thing to note about mixtures, is that because their combination is strictly physical, both pieces retain their same chemical properties. For example, if we get a chance to seperate the water from the table leg, and the salt from the other bowl of water, we'll still have normal salt and a table leg! There's no actual change to either substance, it's just that they mix together in the same spot. Instead of a mixture however, what if we just had a bowl of water with nothing else in it? This is what we call a pure substance; there's only one "thing" in there that doesn't change within itself. If I take a spoon of water from the left side, and a spoon of water from the right, both spoons should hold the exact same thing: water!

Take a read over everything we've talked about so far to get a quick refresher. But in case you're lazy, here's some quick notes:
- Everything is made out of matter.
- There are different physical states of matter (solid, liquid, and gas) that things can exist in.
- Mixtures (homogenous AND heterogenous) are when two seperate pieces of matter are physically combined in some form, and as such can be physically seperated. The chemical properties of these pieces don't change within the mixture as well.
